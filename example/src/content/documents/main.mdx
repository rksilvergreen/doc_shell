<Section id="sec-2-1-1-2-5" title="Registry Maintenance and Reclassification">

The spacecraft registry is maintained by Mission Planning and updated whenever a vessel's role or status changes. Reclassification is required when a craft is repurposed (e.g. from crewed shuttle to cargo-only after human-rating lapse), when a new class is introduced, or when a vessel is retired.

Proposed changes are submitted via the Registry Change Request (RCR) form and reviewed by the Fleet Director. Approval updates the master registry, the mission-assignment rules, and the certification matrix. No vessel may be assigned to a mission type for which it is not currently certified in the registry.

Historical role and status are preserved in the registry archive so that past missions can be correctly attributed. For audit and training, the archive is read-only; only the current snapshot is used for planning and operations.

Registry entries include: designation, role, crew capacity, propulsion system, status, date of last reclassification, and (for retired craft) retirement date and reason. Optional notes may record temporary role changes (e.g. a crewed vessel used in uncrewed mode for a test flight).

At least once per quarter, Mission Planning reconciles the registry against the actual fleet: every active or in-transit vessel must have a current registry entry, and every registry entry must correspond to a real asset or a formally retired vessel. Discrepancies are escalated to the Fleet Director and corrected before the next planning cycle.

<Section id="sec-2-2" title="Design Philosophy">

<Section id="sec-2-4" title="Mass and Payload">

Payload capacity and dry mass determine which missions each vessel can support. Heavy cargo runs use <Link to="row-atlas">Atlas IV</Link>; crew rotations use <Link to="sec-2-1-1">Spacecraft Registry</Link> or, for long-duration, <Link to="row-horizon">Horizon</Link>. Probe missions are mass-limited by the launch vehicle; Voyager X carries only instruments and comms.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-2-5" title="Maintenance and Refit">

Every vessel follows a scheduled maintenance regime. Post-flight inspections cover thermal tiles, propulsion plumbing, and avionics. Life-support filters and battery packs are replaced on fixed intervals; ion drives get thruster wear checks after each long burn.

Major refits are planned between missions. During a refit, subsystems can be upgraded (e.g. swapping an older S-Band transponder for a Ka-Band unit), and structural inspections are performed. The <Link to="row-sentinel">Sentinel</Link> class was retired after refit costs exceeded the value of continued operation; the <Link to="row-meridian">Meridian</Link> fleet now handles the same role with better margins.

Uncrewed craft receive remote diagnostics and, where possible, software and parameter updates. Voyager X cannot be physically serviced but continues to receive command uplinks and trajectory corrections.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-3-2" title="Mission Status">

Every mission progresses through a defined set of statuses. These statuses drive dashboard views, reporting, and automation triggers.

<Section id="sec-3-2-1" title="Mission Status Groups">

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pre-Launch</strong> — Countdown initiated, all systems being verified.</li>
  <li><strong>In Flight</strong> — Vehicle has cleared the launch pad and is in powered ascent or transit.</li>
  <li><strong>Orbit Achieved</strong> — Stable orbit confirmed, payload operations may begin.</li>
  <li><strong>Completed</strong> — Mission objectives fulfilled; vehicle deorbited or parked.</li>
  <li><strong>Aborted</strong> — Mission terminated before completion due to anomaly.</li>
</ul>

<Section id="sec-3-2-1-1" title="Status Transitions">

Status changes are logged and drive reporting and automation.

<Section id="sec-3-2-1-1-1" title="Valid Transitions">

Pre-Launch → In Flight → Orbit Achieved → Completed. Aborted can be entered from any pre-Completed status. No reverse transitions except for data correction.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-3-2-1-1-2" title="Dashboard Display">

Dashboards show current status with color coding; history is available in the mission log for audit.

</Section>

</Section>

Each status has a formal definition used for reporting and compliance.

<Section id="sec-3-2-1-2-1" title="Pre-Launch Through Orbit">

Pre-Launch: countdown in progress. In Flight: vehicle in powered ascent or transit. Orbit Achieved: stable orbit confirmed.

Missions are classified by type for scheduling and resource allocation.

<ul>

  <li><strong>Cargo</strong> — Uncrewed resupply or payload delivery missions.</li>
  <li><strong>Probe</strong> — Autonomous scientific exploration beyond Earth orbit.</li>
  <li><strong>LEO Service</strong> — Satellite deployment, maintenance, or debris cleanup in low Earth orbit.</li>

<Section id="sec-6" title="Operations">

This section describes day-to-day operational procedures, shift handover, communications windows, and emergency protocols. All flight controllers and crew must be familiar with these before assuming duty.

<Section id="sec-6-1" title="Daily Operations">

Mission control runs on a 24/7 schedule during active missions. Shifts are typically eight hours, with a one-hour overlap for handover.

<Section id="sec-6-1-1" title="Shift Structure">

Three shifts per day; handover occurs at shift change.

<Section id="sec-6-1-1-1" title="Handover">

The outgoing shift briefs the incoming shift on status, anomalies, and upcoming events.

<Section id="sec-6-1-1-1-1" title="Handover Checklist">

Required: mission status, open anomalies, next 24 h milestones (burns, comm windows, crew sleep), weather/range constraints. Optional: forward plan, deferred items.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-1-1-1-2" title="Sign-Off">

Outgoing Flight Director signs off only after incoming FD acknowledges and accepts the handover. Log the handover time in the mission log.

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-1-1-2" title="Shift Length and Overlap">

Standard shift is eight hours; one-hour overlap allows joint monitoring during critical events and reduces handover errors.

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-1-2" title="Roles and Authority">

Each shift has defined roles with clear authority boundaries.

<Section id="sec-6-1-2-1" title="Flight Director">

The FD has final say on operational decisions within the mission rules.

<Section id="sec-6-1-2-1-1" title="FD Authority Scope">

FD authority covers: go/no-go for nominal operations, anomaly response within procedures, and escalation to program management when required. FD does not override safety-critical abort criteria.

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-1-2-2" title="CAPCOM and Ground Systems">

CAPCOM handles crew communication; Ground Systems handles tracking and comms. Both report to the FD.

<Section id="sec-6-1-2-2-1" title="Uncrewed Missions">

Uncrewed missions (cargo, probes) have no CAPCOM. Autonomy scripts handle routine ops; ground intervenes for trajectory corrections, payload deployment, and contingencies.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-2" title="Emergency Protocols">

In case of a critical anomaly, the Flight Director may call an emergency hold or abort. Criteria for abort include: loss of redundancy in a critical system, crew injury or medical emergency, trajectory deviation outside safe limits, or loss of communications with a crewed vehicle beyond the allowed outage window.

Abort procedures are vehicle- and phase-specific. During ascent, abort options include Return to Launch Site (RTLS), Abort to Orbit (ATO), or Transatlantic Abort Landing (TAL), depending on time and trajectory. In orbit, the crew may execute an emergency deorbit to a predetermined landing site; mission control coordinates with recovery forces.

All emergency actions must be logged and reported to the program office within 24 hours. A formal mishap investigation is convened for any loss of vehicle, serious injury, or near-miss that could have led to either.

<Section id="sec-6-2-1" title="Abort Types">

Abort options depend on mission phase and vehicle.

<Section id="sec-6-2-1-1" title="Ascent Aborts">

During powered ascent, crewed vehicles may execute RTLS, ATO, or TAL.

<Section id="sec-6-2-1-1-1" title="RTLS">

Return to Launch Site. Used early in ascent when enough propellant remains to reverse and land at the pad.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-2-1-1-2" title="ATO and TAL">

Abort to Orbit: reach a safe orbit, then assess. Transatlantic Abort Landing: land at a predetermined site across the ocean. Choice depends on time and trajectory.

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-2-1-2" title="On-Orbit Emergencies">

In orbit, crew may perform emergency deorbit to a designated landing site. Mission control coordinates with recovery forces.

</Section>

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-3" title="Communications Windows">

Contact with spacecraft depends on ground-station visibility and orbital geometry. Low Earth orbit missions typically have multiple passes per day, each lasting from a few minutes to about fifteen minutes depending on altitude and station location. Deep-space missions have scheduled comm windows; the <Link to="row-horizon">Horizon</Link> and <Link to="row-voyager-x">Voyager X</Link> assets use the Deep Space Network (DSN) for long-range links.

During a pass, priority is given to telemetry ingestion, command uplink (if needed), and voice or data exchange with crew. Non-critical uploads (e.g. software patches, science plans) are queued and sent when bandwidth allows. Missed windows are reported; repeated misses trigger a review of antenna scheduling and vehicle health.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-4" title="Logistics and Resupply">

Cargo missions to the station are planned on a regular cadence. The <Link to="row-atlas">Atlas IV</Link> fleet handles the majority of resupply; each flight is manifested with consumables (water, oxygen, food, propellant), spare parts, and science payloads. Stowage is optimized for mass and center-of-gravity limits; loading is verified before closure of the cargo module.

Return cargo (samples, failed hardware, trash) is loaded for disposal or analysis on Earth. Mass and volume on return legs are often more constrained than on ascent, so prioritization is agreed between the station crew and ground.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-6-5" title="Reporting and Metrics">

Daily mission summaries are distributed to program management and stakeholders. They include: status (per <Link to="sec-3-2-1">Mission Status Groups</Link>), key events, anomalies, and forward plan. Weekly and monthly reports aggregate reliability metrics, propellant usage, and schedule performance.

All reports are stored in the program repository. Auditors and safety reviewers have read-only access to logs and reports for the life of the program.

</Section>

</Section>

<Section id="sec-7" title="Training">

Personnel assigned to flight operations or crew must complete the appropriate certification track. Training is updated when procedures or vehicles change.

<Section id="sec-7-1" title="Flight Controller Certification">

Flight controllers complete classroom training on systems (power, propulsion, life support, etc.), then simulation runs covering nominal and off-nominal cases. Certification is signed off by the discipline lead and the Flight Director. Recertification is required annually and after any major procedure or vehicle change.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-7-2" title="Crew Training">

Crew train on vehicle systems, emergency procedures, and mission-specific tasks (EVA, science ops, docking). They also participate in joint simulations with mission control. Crew are not certified for flight until all required runs are completed and the crew office concurs.

</Section>

<Section id="sec-7-3" title="Training Records">

All certifications and recertifications are recorded in the training database. Audits ensure that only qualified personnel are assigned to active missions.

</Section>
